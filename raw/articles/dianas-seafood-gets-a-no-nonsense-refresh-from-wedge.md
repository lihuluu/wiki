[Wedge’s](https://www.wedge.work/) packaging for Diana’s makes a bold statement with oversized typography and an unapologetic use of color. The deep blue and warm yellow contrast against a crisp white background, giving the seafood brand a strong shelf presence.

The layout leans into a no-frills, straightforward approach, making the product feel high quality while maintaining approachability. The stacked type treatment on the oyster boxes adds a playful rhythm, while the clean, grid-based design of the pouches ensures clarity and impact. It’s simple but effective, rooted in the brand’s four-decade history.

Sarah Di Domenico, Co-Founder and Chief Creative Officer of [Wedge](https://www.wedge.work/), tells us more about how the design came to be below.

---

**What was the client’s brief for this project?**

*“Design a consumer brand that makes a line of quality seafood products desirable for everyone and distinct in the category.”*

Diana’s Seafood started as a fishmonger shop and supplier in the 1970s, built on a reputation for exceptional quality, obsessive sourcing, and deep expertise in seafood.

Over the decades, it became a go-to destination for chefs and home cooks alike. Matthew Corbeth, Head of Brand at Diana’s, saw an opportunity to extend that legacy beyond the shop. The idea was simple: Bring the same premium seafood Diana’s was known for into retail for the masses. The challenge was to honor the brand’s deep-rooted history while introducing something fresh and modern. And do it with a new brand that had zero awareness in the category. It had to stand out, feel iconic, and immediately signal quality to a new generation of seafood lovers.

![](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-2b-scaled.jpg?w=2011&quality=70&ssl=1)

**How did you execute the brief and what were some of the strategic design elements that you built into the brand?**

The name *Diana’s* had to be instantly recognizable, so we designed a strong wordmark that stands proud and bold on every pack, ensuring visibility from across the shelf (just like a name on a boat across the harbor).

We createddelicious food photography — to convey the quality and taste. Product names are set in Milano typeface, designed by Alex Lescieux, is a variable font that brings personality and color to the brand system. On the back of the packaging, hand-drawn illustrations by Bill Rebholz give a nod to Diana’s Mediterranean roots, adding warmth and charm.  
  
[![](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-1.jpg?resize=1024%2C683&quality=70&ssl=1)](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-1.jpg?resize=1024%2C683&quality=70&ssl=1)

**I’d imagine capturing front-of-pack-worthy photography of seafood isn’t the easiest task. Can you walk me through this process and what the most difficult part of it was / how you handled it?**  
  
With our experience in retail, we know the power of showing the product—when done right, it drives conversion. And with a lineup like this—a skin-on, boneless Mediterranean Sea Bass fillet with Kalamata olives, sun-dried tomatoes, parsley, garlic, and a squeeze of lemon (yum!)—we needed to do it justice.

But seafood *is* tricky. It’s delicate. And clam chowder is… clam chowder. It needs to look fresh, nourishing, and delicious—not overly styled or over-promising. So we took a very simple direction to spark the senses. One of the challenges we faced was to get the colors right—early print tests came out cold and desaturated so we had to adjust all the photos to get the right vibrancy and warmth so the food looks appetizing!

![](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-5b.png?w=1500&quality=65&ssl=1)

**Can you walk me through the typographic hierarchy of the packaging design? Where do you hope a consumer begins their journey with it, and what do you hope it communicates?**  
  
With both English and French on the pack, the system had to be flexible enough to accommodate a variety of product names and descriptions while maintaining clarity and impact. The solution was to establish two primary levels of information. First off, the wordmark is designed with *DIANA’S* as large as possible to build brand recognition. Consumers need to become familiar with the name, while *Seafood* is secondary—after all, you’re already in the seafood aisle!

Secondly, the product name is the hero. Set in Milano, the name is the most expressive element after the brand. Its rich, warm-red, and dynamic typography adds character while ensuring visibility, making each product instantly recognizable.

***CASE STUDY***

*For over 45 years, Diana’s Seafood has built a solid reputation on an unshakeable principle: “Do it right or make it right”. That relentless commitment to quality made them the gold standard supplier for Canada’s top chefs and Michelin-starred kitchens. But the next horizon? Your kitchen.*

*Diana’s set out to redefine what we expect from seafood at home—bringing premium, true-to-label, responsibly sourced fish onto the family dinner table through a line of delicious meals. To do that, they needed more than just packaging. They needed a big brand vision and world with a strategic foundation that would shake perception and bring a new experience to the deep-seated traditions of the seafood category.*

![](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-2-scaled.jpg?resize=2048%2C1366&quality=70&ssl=1)

*Enter Wedge.*

*Where most institutions have a founding father, Diana’s Seafood had something different. A founding mother. “The Mother of Seafood” became the north star—a brand platform that is as bold as it is warm. A symbol of trust, nourishment, and care. If it’s Diana’s, it’s good!*

*The system is anchored by the blue logo on the right side, standing bold and confident, inspired by vernacular of harbour boats. Letterforms, specifically the S, feature custom details that echo the wave of the sea. The terminals of the S are also used to create a ‘frame’ detail around the photography. The symbol acts as a ‘stamp of quality’, featuring two fish, the shapes resembling a D and an S (for Diana’s Seafood).*

![](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-15-Before-After-scaled.jpg?resize=2048%2C1365&quality=70&ssl=1)

![](https://i0.wp.com/thedieline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Dianas-Wedge-9-scaled.jpg?resize=2048%2C1366&quality=70&ssl=1)

*The color palette is vibrant, inviting, and modern, bringing a warm and nourishing energy to the competitive shelf that’s primarily white, navy, and shades of blue. The secondary typeface brings in a language of quality, authenticity, and care while the illustration drawn by American artist Bill Rebholz, brings a distinctive European touch and charm to the world of Diana’s.*

*“The Mother of Seafood” is the bigger idea that became the foundation for a brand world that radiates confidence, embraces joy, and sets a new standard for what responsibly sourced seafood looks like.*
